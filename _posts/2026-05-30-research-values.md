What do you actually value in research — not what you'd write in a statement, but what you'd *trade*?

At a lab retreat last year, Charlotte Range had us play a research-themed *Would You Rather*: doubled pay on a topic you hate, or half the pay on one you love? World expert on a narrow niche, or a versatile generalist who's a master of none? The answers were forgettable; the *reasons* were not. People argued, conceded, surprised themselves. That's the whole idea — a value is what you're willing to sacrifice for, and a forced choice is the cheapest way to find out which one is really yours.

So I built a small web version, and it's live: **[Research Values](/tools/research-values/)**.

It has two modes. A scored one maps your picks onto ten research trade-offs — curiosity vs real-world impact, rigor vs novelty, depth vs breadth, autonomy vs security, integrity vs expedience, and five more — and draws you a "research-values fingerprint," flagging the axes where you genuinely split as *live tensions* rather than pretending to resolve them. And an icebreaker mode: two options, random order, click through, argue out loud — built for your own retreats and seminars.

A few things I held to. There are no wrong answers; it's a reflective mirror, not a personality test. A field selector means a wet-lab biologist and a pure mathematician aren't handed the same questions — each field even gets its own native axis. And nothing leaves your browser: no accounts, no backend, no analytics.

Play it, then tell me which card is wrong and which one's missing — that feedback is the next version. Credit where it's due: the original card game is Charlotte Range's.
